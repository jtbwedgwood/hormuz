# Foundation Chronology And Scenarios

Last updated: 2026-07-06. Scope: event chronology and baseline/scenario framing for `hormuz-fyp.2` and `hormuz-fyp.3`.

## Current Read

As of 2026-07-06, the Strait of Hormuz is not back to normal. The best working description is "partially reopening after a de facto closure," with traffic recovering unevenly, mine/attack risk still material, and the U.S.-Iran 60-day arrangement not yet a stable governance settlement. Use official EIA assumptions as the central energy-market baseline: near-term effective closure/limited shipping, gradual oil-shipment resumption in 3Q26, and no return to pre-conflict traffic until early 2027.

## Scenario Taxonomy

| Scenario | Definition | Observable triggers | Working assumptions | Key outputs |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| S0 Normal/recovered baseline | Traffic and flows return to pre-2026 patterns; war-risk premiums normalize. | Daily vessel counts within 90-110% of baseline for 30 days; no UKMTO substantial/high threat; no abnormal anchorage congestion. | Use as counterfactual for costs and tracker charts. | Normal daily ships, normal flow volumes, baseline prices/freight. |
| S1 Insurance/security shock | Strait remains legally open, but attacks, mines, GNSS interference, naval presence, or crew/insurer refusal reduces traffic and raises costs. | UKMTO warnings, war-risk premium spikes, rerouting south of TSS/Omani waters, anchorages growing, delayed voyages. | Physical supply may move with delays and higher freight; price impact concentrated in products, freight, and regional spreads. | Delay days, effective capacity haircut, insurance/freight adders, product-price passthrough. |
| S2 Partial closure/managed corridor | Some traffic allowed through preferred corridors, flags, cargoes, or negotiated windows; other vessels wait or reroute. | Confirmed crossings below 70% baseline; route selection shifts; official corridor/toll/control statements; persistent congestion. | Treat throughput as stochastic and politically constrained, not zero. | Daily allowed throughput, backlog, commodity priority ranking, importer exposure. |
| S3 De facto full closure | Commercial transits mostly stop because ship operators, insurers, or militaries deem passage too risky even if no formal legal closure is recognized. | Daily crossings near zero or only military/Iranian/special cases; P&I/war-risk withdrawal; large vessel queues on both sides. | Use 2026-02-28 onward early shock as template. Pipeline bypass and inventory draw become binding. | Supply removed/delayed, inventory draw, price shock, demand destruction. |
| S4 Formal closure/blockade | A state formally declares or enforces closure/blockade against broad vessel categories. | Official notices, interdictions/boardings, mine-laying confirmation, naval blockade enforcement. | Highest legal/geopolitical escalation; commercial data may degrade because AIS goes dark. | Maximum disruption case; separate verified transits from AIS-dark/unknown. |
| S5 Rerouting-delay adaptation | Operators avoid parts of Hormuz/TSS, wait offshore, use Saudi/UAE pipelines, or substitute non-Gulf supply. | Omani/southern corridor use, Red Sea/Yanbu/Fujairah flows, U.S. export surge, Asian replacement cargoes. | Does not remove all supply, but increases voyage times, freight, refinery mismatch, and product cracks. | Delay-adjusted barrels, route maps, substitution matrix. |
| S6 Fragile reopening | Diplomatic agreement creates partial recovery, but threat level and governance/toll disputes remain. | Rising crossings but below baseline; renewed warnings/attacks; toll or corridor disputes; output restoration announcements. | Current best-fit scenario as of 2026-07-06. Model with high reversal risk until at least the 60-day clock from 2026-06-17 expires. | Recovery path, confidence bands, event-conditioned price scenarios. |

## Implementation Notes

- Day 0 for shock charts: 2026-02-28. For pre-event baselines, end windows on 2026-02-27.
- Treat EIA STEO as the central public energy-market source, not as ground truth. Label its figures as assumptions/forecasts when used for scenarios.
- Keep "traffic," "flow," "production shut-in," and "available supply" distinct. A ship-count drop is not mechanically equal to barrels removed because vessel size, cargo type, load status, and bypass/substitution matter.
- Use confidence labels: high = official/statistical source with clear definition; medium = reputable news or official statement reported secondhand; low = unsourced tracker/social snippet or inferred operational detail.
- Current largest uncertainties: actual daily transits by vessel class; AIS-dark traffic; exact war-risk insurance cost; unsold barrels versus delayed barrels; enforceability of the 2026-06-17 U.S.-Iran arrangement; whether toll/corridor disputes revive closure conditions before mid-August 2026.
