# Define normalized comparison metrics

## Description

Develop metrics that compare shocks rigorously: disrupted share of global supply, spare capacity, inventory cover, demand elasticity, route substitutability, strategic reserve context, and price response.

## Acceptance Criteria

Metrics avoid simple nominal barrel comparisons and can be computed for current and historical cases.

### Case Universe Stress Tests

Use these as stress tests from the selected `hormuz-4j7.1` case universe: 1956 Suez, 1967-75 Suez closure, 1973-74 Arab oil embargo, 1978-79 Iranian revolution, 1980 Iran-Iraq outbreak and Tanker War context, 1990-91 Iraq/Kuwait Gulf crisis, 2002-03 Venezuela strike, 2003 Iraq/Nigeria/Venezuela compound shock, 2005 Katrina/Rita, 2011 Libya disruption, 2019 Abqaiq/Khurais attack, 2021 Ever Given/Suez, 2022 Russia/Ukraine sanctions and buyer strike, and the current Hormuz disruption. COVID-19 should remain a method/macro context case rather than a core supply-shock comparator unless a later task explicitly needs it.

The comparison should avoid "million barrels per day lost" rankings unless the barrels are normalized by market size, available buffers, duration, substitution options, and price response. For non-oil cases, keep parallel units but preserve the same logic: share of relevant supply/trade exposed, buffer days, substitutability, duration, and delivered-cost response.

### Normalized Metric Framework

| Metric | Definition | Unit | Denominator/window | Recommended source families | Confidence caveats | Works for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Disrupted share of global supply/trade | Flow directly lost, delayed, rerouted, or at high risk divided by normal global production, consumption, or seaborne trade for the relevant commodity. Use a separate "exposed" version for at-risk flows and "realized disruption" version for confirmed lost/delayed flows. | Percent; plus native flow units | Baseline: trailing 12 months or same-season 3-year average before shock. Shock window: daily/weekly where possible; monthly if historical data are sparse. | EIA International Energy Statistics, EIA chokepoint briefs, IEA Oil Market Report/Gas Market Report, JODI, OPEC Annual Statistical Bulletin, UN Comtrade/BACI, Kpler/Vortexa/MarineTraffic/Lloyd's List for flows, IFA/FAOSTAT/USGS for fertilizer minerals. | Biggest risk is mixing production loss, export loss, and transit exposure. Historical embargoes often affected allocation and prices more than physical global supply. AIS-derived rerouting can double-count delayed cargoes unless vessel IDs/cargoes are reconciled. | Oil: high. Gas/LNG: medium-high. Shipping: high for tonnage/TEU or vessel-days. Fertilizer: medium. |
| Disruption intensity | Average disrupted flow as a share of baseline, multiplied by duration; captures both depth and persistence. | Percent-days, mb/d-days, bcm-days, TEU-days, tonne-days | Compute from first material deviation to recovery below threshold; recommend thresholds of 5%, 10%, and 20% below baseline for sensitivity. | Same as disrupted share; add historical chronologies from IEA emergency response publications, EIA country briefs, CRS/CFR/academic event studies. | Recovery date is judgment-heavy: prices may normalize before physical flows, or vice versa. For COVID, demand collapse and logistics bottlenecks need separate signs. | All. |
| Spare capacity buffer | Rapidly available substitute supply divided by disrupted flow, and rapid spare capacity as share of global demand. For oil, use capacity that can be brought online quickly and sustained; for LNG/gas, use flexible liquefaction, pipeline imports, storage withdrawal deliverability, and demand response; for shipping, use spare vessel capacity and alternative route capacity. | Ratio; percent of demand; days to activate | Pre-shock month/quarter; activation horizon should be stated, preferably 30/90 days for oil and season-specific for gas. | EIA Short-Term Energy Outlook/spare capacity estimates, IEA OMR, OPEC data, company/government capacity reports, GIIGNL/IEA LNG data, ENTSOG/AGSI for Europe, Drewry/Clarksons/Alphaliner for shipping capacity. | Spare capacity is not just nameplate capacity. Political willingness, crude quality, sanctions, port limits, and insurance can make "paper spare" unusable. For historical cases, real-time estimates may differ from revised estimates. | Oil: high. Gas/LNG: medium. Shipping: medium. Fertilizer: low-medium. |
| Inventory cover | Usable inventories divided by normal daily consumption, refinery runs, imports, exports, or disrupted flow. Report commercial and strategic/government stocks separately. | Days of cover; percent of annual demand | Stock level immediately before shock; use monthly/weekly nearest observation. For seasonal products, compare to same-season historical range. | IEA oil stocks and emergency response data, EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report and days-of-supply series, JODI oil/gas stocks, AGSI+ gas storage, national energy ministries, USDA/industry inventory data for fertilizers where available. | Inventory may be in the wrong location, grade, or ownership category. Strategic stocks are policy-dependent and cannot all be drawn at maximum rate. LNG storage is often operational rather than seasonal outside Europe. | Oil: high. Gas/LNG: medium-high. Shipping: low except port/container backlogs. Fertilizer: low-medium. |
| Strategic stockpile response | Public stock release, mandated industry stock draw, demand restraint, fuel switching, or emergency sharing divided by disrupted flow and by total public stock. | mb/d equivalent; percent of disruption; percent of public stock; days offset | Announced and actual release by week/month during shock. Separate announcement effect from physical draw. | IEA collective action notices, IEA oil security pages, DOE SPR release history, national reserve agencies, IEA gas security work, government fertilizer/food security agencies. | Announced barrels may be optioned, exchanged, or sold over time rather than immediately delivered. China and non-IEA stock data can be opaque. Need avoid treating all SPR release as net incremental supply if it replaces commercial draws. | Oil: high. Gas/LNG: medium. Shipping: not applicable except emergency routing/capacity measures. Fertilizer: low. |
| Demand elasticity / demand destruction | Observed or modeled demand change attributable to price/supply shock, normalized by price change and baseline demand. Report short-run elasticity and realized demand destruction separately. | Elasticity; percent demand change; mb/d, bcm, tonnes | Short-run: event window of 1-3 months; medium-run: 6-12 months. Use counterfactual baseline from pre-shock forecast where possible. | IEA OMR/GMR demand revisions, EIA STEO, IMF/World Bank macro data, academic elasticity estimates, national consumption data, airline/road fuel demand proxies, industrial output data. | Attribution is hard during recessions/wars/COVID because income, mobility, policy, and supply constraints move together. Elasticity estimates are product- and horizon-specific. | Oil: medium-high. Gas/LNG: medium. Shipping: medium through trade volumes. Fertilizer: medium but crop-cycle lag matters. |
| Route/substitution constraint | Share of disrupted/exposed flow that can be rerouted or substituted without binding capacity, quality, regulatory, or delivery-time constraints. Include extra distance/time and capacity of bypass routes. | Percent substitutable; days delay; nautical miles; added $/unit | Baseline route and destination matrix before shock; evaluate during first 30/60/90 days and full shock. | EIA/IEA chokepoint and country briefs, port/terminal capacity data, pipeline operator/government capacity, AIS providers, UNCTAD maritime data, Lloyd's List, Clarksons, Drewry, GIIGNL LNG terminal data, UN Comtrade for source substitution. | A barrel/cargo can be technically reroutable but commercially unavailable because of contracts, sanctions, quality mismatch, refinery configuration, destination port limits, or war-risk insurance. | Oil: high. Gas/LNG: high. Shipping: high. Fertilizer: medium-high. |
| Price response | Change in relevant spot/futures/delivered price against pre-shock baseline, inflation-adjusted when comparing across history. Include peak, average over event, and persistence after recovery. | Percent; real $/bbl, $/MMBtu, $/tonne, freight index points | Baseline: 20 trading days, 3 months, and trailing 12 months before shock. Event: peak within 30/90 days; average during disruption; 30/90-day post-recovery. | World Bank Pink Sheet, IMF Primary Commodity Prices, EIA/ICE/CME price series, FRED/CPI deflators, Argus/Platts where licensed, Baltic Exchange/Drewry/SCFI for freight, USDA/DTN/World Bank for fertilizer. | Price response reflects expectations, inventories, macro demand, policy, exchange rates, and monetary conditions, not just physical loss. Nominal prices are not comparable across decades; use real prices and percent deviations. | All. |
| Price per unit of disruption | Price change divided by disrupted share or disruption intensity. Designed as a rough "market tightness sensitivity" indicator, not a causal estimate. | Percent price change per 1% supply loss; real $ per percent-day | Same event windows as price response and disruption intensity. | Combine price and disruption sources; academic event studies for robustness. | Highly sensitive to expectations and confounders. Should be shown with uncertainty bands and never as a standalone ranking. | Oil: medium. Gas/LNG: medium. Shipping: medium. Fertilizer: low-medium. |
| Duration and recovery shape | Time from onset to trough, partial recovery, full physical recovery, and price normalization. Categorize as spike, plateau, rolling disruption, or regime shift. | Days/weeks/months; categorical shape | Onset definition must be case-specific but documented; recovery thresholds should use 90%, 95%, and 100% of baseline sensitivity. | Event chronologies from IEA/EIA, Reuters/AP/FT archives, government reports, AIS flow data, price series. | Some shocks have no clean end date; COVID and sanctions are regime changes more than one-off shocks. Physical recovery and price recovery can diverge. | All. |
| Geographic concentration | Concentration of disrupted supply origins, exposed transit routes, import destinations, and reserve buffers. | HHI; top-3/top-5 share; percent import dependence | Pre-shock annual flow matrix; optionally same-season quarter. | UN Comtrade/BACI, EIA/IEA/JODI country flows, Kpler/Vortexa, customs data, IMF Direction of Trade, IFA/USGS for fertilizer inputs. | Country-level flows can hide firm-level contracts, grades, and cargo swaps. Sanctions and transshipment obscure origin/destination. | Oil: high. Gas/LNG: high. Shipping: high. Fertilizer: high. |
| Shipping/insurance channel | Added delivered cost and friction from war-risk premiums, freight rates, rerouting, port congestion, demurrage, security escort, and cargo cancellation. | $/unit; percent delivered-cost uplift; voyage days; war-risk premium percent; vessel-days lost | Daily/weekly during shock; compare to pre-shock 20-day and 3-month averages. | UNCTAD Review of Maritime Transport, Baltic Exchange, Drewry, Clarksons, Lloyd's List, IUMI/insurance broker notes, AIS providers, port authority data. | War-risk and charter fixtures can be proprietary. Public freight indices may not match the affected route or vessel class. Insurance costs are often quoted as ranges and may be bundled into freight. | Oil: high. Gas/LNG: high. Shipping: high. Fertilizer: medium-high. |
| Product criticality / downstream exposure | Importance of the disrupted product to downstream sectors and ability to substitute final-use demand. For oil products, distinguish crude, condensate, diesel, jet, gasoline, LPG, naphtha. For fertilizer, distinguish nitrogen/ammonia/urea, phosphate, potash, sulfur. | Sector share; percent input cost; exposed final demand; qualitative substitutability score | Annual input-output baseline; shock window for price passthrough. | OECD/BEA/WIOD input-output tables, IEA product balances, FAO/USDA fertilizer use, World Bank/IMF commodity data, national statistics. | Sector exposure is not the same as immediate shortage. Contract lags, hedging, and regulated prices delay passthrough. | Oil: high. Gas/LNG: high. Shipping: medium. Fertilizer: high. |

For each selected historical/current case, collect these minimum fields:

| Field | Purpose |

### Methodological Anchors and Source Breadcrumbs

- Source quality: use `docs/foundation-research-standards.md` from `hormuz-fyp.1`; prefer official statistics and operational data, label confidence, and archive query/version details.
- Chokepoint denominator: EIA's Strait of Hormuz brief reports 2024 oil flow through the strait at about 20 million b/d, roughly 20% of global petroleum liquids consumption, and its World Oil Transit Chokepoints page frames chokepoints as critical narrow channels on global sea routes. Sources: EIA Today in Energy, "Amid regional conflict, the Strait of Hormuz remains critical oil chokepoint" (2025-06-16), https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=65504; EIA, "World Oil Transit Chokepoints", https://www.eia.gov/international/analysis/special-topics/world_oil_transit_Chokepoints.
- Spare capacity definition: EIA defines surplus/spare crude capacity as effective production capacity minus actual production and emphasizes capacity withheld under OPEC/OPEC+ agreements rather than disrupted production. Source: EIA Today in Energy, "EIA updates its definitions and estimates of OPEC crude oil production capacity" (2026-01-09), https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=66904.
- Inventory cover: EIA describes crude days of supply as commercial crude inventories divided by forecast refinery runs, excluding government-held SPR inventories. Source: EIA Today in Energy, "U.S. commercial crude oil inventories now provide the most days of supply..." (2015-03-24), https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=20432; EIA petroleum data portal, https://www.eia.gov/petroleum/data.php.
- Strategic stocks: IEA member countries have a 90-day net import stockholding obligation and collective response tools; IEA's oil stock data tool defines the 90-day commitment as daily net imports times 90. Sources: IEA, "Oil security and emergency response", https://www.iea.org/about/oil-security-and-emergency-response; IEA, "Oil Stocks of IEA Countries", https://www.iea.org/data-and-statistics/data-tools/oil-stocks-of-iea-countries.
- Historical emergency response benchmark: IEA's 2011 Libya release made 60 million barrels available over one month; IEA notes previous collective actions in 1991, 2005, 2011, and twice in 2022. Sources: IEA, "IEA makes 60 million barrels of oil available to market to offset Libyan disruption" (2011-06-23), https://www.iea.org/news/iea-makes-60-million-barrels-of-oil-available-to-market-to-offset-libyan-disruption; IEA, "IEA Member countries to carry out largest ever oil stock release..." (2026-03-11), https://www.iea.org/news/iea-member-countries-to-carry-out-largest-ever-oil-stock-release-amid-market-disruptions-from-middle-east-conflict.
- Price series: World Bank Pink Sheet provides monthly commodity prices and historical monthly/annual workbooks; IMF Primary Commodity Prices provides commodity price data and country-specific commodity-price indices. Sources: World Bank Commodity Markets, https://www.worldbank.org/en/research/commodity-markets; IMF Primary Commodity Markets, https://www.imf.org/en/research/commodity-prices.
- Oil demand elasticity anchor: Caldara, Cavallo, and Iacoviello estimate short-run oil supply and demand elasticities around 0.08 and -0.08 using narrative/instrumental-variable methods; use as a methodological caution, not a universal parameter. Source: "Oil price elasticities and oil price fluctuations", https://www.matteoiacoviello.com/research_files/CCI.pdf.
- Shipping/chokepoint methodology: UNCTAD's Review of Maritime Transport is the long-running structural/cyclical source family for seaborne trade, ports, freight, and shipping costs; recent chokepoint research explicitly models delays, rerouting, insurance premiums, trade disruption, and freight costs. Sources: UNCTAD Review of Maritime Transport series, https://shop.un.org/books/series/review-maritime-transport; Verschuur et al., "Systemic impacts of disruptions at maritime chokepoints", Nature Communications (2025), https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-65403-w.
- Gas/LNG storage and flexibility: IEA gas security work emphasizes gas storage and flexibility along gas/LNG value chains; EIA weekly gas storage provides U.S. working gas stocks as a high-frequency source, while AGSI+ is the European operational storage source. Sources: IEA, "Secure gas and LNG value chains call for greater international co-operation" (2024), https://www.iea.org/commentaries/secure-gas-and-lng-value-chains-call-for-greater-international-co-operation; EIA Weekly Natural Gas Storage Report, https://www.eia.gov/naturalgas/storage/.
- Fertilizer linkage: USDA ERS identifies natural gas as a key input for ammonia/nitrogen fertilizer and documents historical production/import adjustment under gas price pressure; use this as a template for downstream exposure and substitution constraints. Source: USDA ERS, "Impact of Rising Natural Gas Prices on U.S. Ammonia Supply" (2007), https://www.ers.usda.gov/media/9339/wrs-0702.pdf.
